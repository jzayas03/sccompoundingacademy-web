## MODULE 1 — Introduction to Pharmaceutical Compounding

Create an educational presentation titled **"Introduction to Pharmaceutical Compounding"** for pharmacists, pharmacy students, and healthcare professionals in Puerto Rico.

**Slide 1 — Title Slide**
"Introduction to Pharmaceutical Compounding" | Santa Cruz Compounding Academy | 15 años de servicio

**Slide 2 — What Is Compounding?**
Define pharmaceutical compounding as the art and science of preparing personalized medications for individual patients. Explain why it exists: when a patient needs a different dose, dosage form, flavor, or when a commercial product is unavailable or on the FDA Drug Shortage List. Under **Section 503A** of the FD&C Act, state-licensed pharmacies compound patient-specific prescriptions; under **Section 503B**, registered outsourcing facilities may compound without a patient-specific prescription for hospital and clinical use.

**Slide 3 — History of Compounding**
From ancient apothecaries to modern pharmacies. Compounding was the core of pharmacy practice before the industrial pharmaceutical era. Its resurgence in the late 20th century driven by patient-specific needs. Landmark regulatory events: DQSA 2013 (Drug Quality and Security Act) creating 503A/503B framework. FDA's 2024 proposed rule on "Demonstrable Difficulties for Compounding" (DDC lists) as the latest regulatory evolution affecting which drugs may be compounded.

**Slide 4 — Who Benefits from Compounded Medications?**
Three patient groups: (1) Patients needing unique doses or dosage forms — pediatric liquid from adult tablet. (2) Patients with allergies to commercial dyes, preservatives, or excipients. (3) Patients needing discontinued or back-ordered drugs — especially relevant given ongoing drug shortages. Examples: a child who needs a mango-flavored suspension, a woman who needs a hormone cream at a custom concentration via TOPI-CLICK, a patient needing an alternative dosage form due to dysphagia.

**Slide 5 — USP 795: Non-Sterile Compounding Standards (Revised November 2023)**
Cover the **major changes effective November 1, 2023**: facility requirements, designated person (DP) requirement (new), personnel training and competency documentation, beyond-use dating now based on **water activity (aW)** instead of "water-containing" terminology.
Updated BUD Table (USP 795, 2023):
- Aqueous dosage forms (aW ≥ 0.60): **14 days refrigerated; 35 days frozen**
- Non-aqueous dosage forms (aW < 0.60): **90 days at controlled room temperature; 180 days refrigerated**
- Solid dosage forms: **180 days at controlled room temperature**
- With CNSP-specific stability data: up to **180 days maximum**
Note: BUDs may be extended beyond Table 4 limits only if supported by stability-indicating analytical testing.

**Slide 6 — USP 797: Sterile Compounding Standards (Revised November 2023)**
**Major restructuring effective November 1, 2023**: eliminated low/medium/high-risk categories. Now uses **Category 1, Category 2, and Category 3 CSPs** based on compounding environment and controls — not preparation complexity.
- **Category 1 CSP** (ISO 5 PEC, uncontrolled area): BUD **12 hours RT / 24 hours refrigerated**
- **Category 2 CSP** (ISO 5 PEC in ISO 7 buffer room + ISO 8 anteroom): BUD ranges from 1 day to **90 days** depending on sterility testing and starting components
- **Category 3 CSP** (full cleanroom suite + sterility testing + weekly environmental monitoring): BUD **60–180 days**
Environmental sampling: viable air sampling minimum every **6 months** for Cat 1/2; within **30 days** before commencing Cat 3.

**Slide 7 — USP 800: Hazardous Drug Handling (Compendially Enforceable November 2023)**
USP 800 became compendially enforceable **November 1, 2023** alongside revised 795/797. NIOSH Hazardous Drug List now in **two tables**: Table 1 (drugs with carcinogenic/reproductive risk + manufacturer safe handling guidance) and Table 2 (hazardous per NIOSH but without Table 1 criteria). **Assessment of Risk (AOR)** required for all drugs on NIOSH list. Full containment controls mandatory for all antineoplastic Table 1 drugs. Organizations may conduct AOR for non-antineoplastic drugs to determine alternate containment strategies. PPE requirements, negative-pressure C-SEC with C-PEC, HEPA filtration, routine wipe testing especially relevant for hormone and chemotherapy compounds at Santa Cruz.

**Slide 8 — Puerto Rico Regulatory Framework**
Junta de Farmacia de PR rules, prescription requirements for magistral formulas. Federal FDCA Section 503A (patient-specific, licensed pharmacy) vs. Section 503B (outsourcing facility, hospital distribution). FDA 2024 proposed DDC rule — awareness required for compounders. Regulatory compliance at Santa Cruz Pharma Care as a 503A entity.

**Slide 9 — Pharmaceutical Bases and Vehicles: The Compounder's Toolkit**
Overview of: creams (O/W and W/O emulsions), gels, ointments, suspensions, capsule bases. How vehicle selection (water activity, lipophilicity, viscosity) affects drug delivery, onset, patient compliance, and BUD assignment under the updated USP 795 water activity framework.

## MODULE 2 — Dermatology Compounding

Create an educational presentation titled **"Dermatology Compounding at Santa Cruz Pharma Care"** for dermatologists, aesthetic practitioners, and pharmacists in Puerto Rico.

**Slide 1 — Title**
"Dermatology Compounding: Custom Solutions for Every Skin Type" | Santa Cruz Compounding Academy

**Slide 2 — Why Dermatology Compounding?**
Commercial products are one-size-fits-all. Compounding allows: custom concentrations of active ingredients, combination of multiple actives in one vehicle, preservative-free or dye-free formulas, bases optimized for skin type. Key conditions treated: melasma, hyperpigmentation, acne, anti-aging, psoriasis, eczema, nail fungus, wound healing.

**Slide 3 — Hydroquinone (HQ): the Gold Standard Depigmenting Agent**
Mechanism: reversible tyrosinase inhibition → reduced melanin synthesis. Compounded concentrations: 2%–8%. Applications: melasma, solar lentigines, post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH).
**2025 Updates (AAD, EADV, International Delphi Consensus 2025):**
- HQ is no longer available OTC in the US — **prescription required** for all concentrations.
- Triple combination therapy (HQ + tretinoin + corticosteroid = Kligman formula) remains the **gold standard** per 2025 international Delphi consensus (40 dermatologists, 11 countries).
- Broad-spectrum SPF 50+ sunscreen is **essential** — all wavelengths including visible light can induce melasma.
- Prolonged HQ use can cause **exogenous ochronosis** — monitor for this, especially in darker skin tones (Fitzpatrick IV–VI).
- Treatment cycles: 3–4 months on, monitor, cycle off as needed.

**Slide 4 — Kligman Formula: Triple Combination Therapy**
HQ + tretinoin + corticosteroid (e.g., fluocinolone acetonide 0.01%). Synergistic mechanism: HQ blocks melanin synthesis, tretinoin increases cell turnover and penetration of HQ, steroid reduces inflammation and irritation.
**2025 update:** Individualize steroid potency to skin type; use lower-potency corticosteroids for longer treatment cycles. Compounded as cream or gel per prescriber preference. Patient counseling: start at night, expect dryness/irritation weeks 1–2, mandatory SPF 50+, and tinted sunscreen for visible light protection. Custom concentrations, fresher batches, and lower cost vs. commercial Tri-Luma.

**Slide 5 — Alternative Depigmenting Agents (HQ-Free)**
Kojic acid 1%–4%, azelaic acid 10%–20%, **oral tranexamic acid 250 mg BID** (emerging first-line in pregnancy alternatives per 2025 expert consensus), topical tranexamic acid 3%–5%, niacinamide 4%–10%, arbutin 1%–2%. When used: pregnancy, HQ sensitivity, maintenance phase, combination therapy. Can be combined in a single compounded formula.

**Slide 6 — Tretinoin and Retinoids in Compounding**
Concentrations: 0.025%–0.1% cream or gel. Mechanism: increases cell turnover, stimulates collagen, reduces comedones. Managing the "purge phase" (weeks 2–6). Photodegradation — amber packaging and refrigeration critical. Combined with niacinamide and ceramides to minimize irritation. **2025 update:** Retinoids and hydroxy acids seldom used as monotherapy for melasma — almost exclusively in combination treatments per current dermatology expert consensus.

**Slide 7 — Custom Acne Formulations**
Ingredients: benzoyl peroxide 2.5%–10%, clindamycin phosphate 1%–2%, niacinamide 4%, salicylic acid 0.5%–2%. Benefits of compounded combos: reduced irritation, targeted strengths, no preservative reactions. Gel vs. cream for oily vs. dry skin types.

**Slide 8 — Anti-Aging and Skin Repair Compounds**
Peptide complexes (Argireline, Matrixyl), vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid, stability requires pH 2.5–3.5 and exclusion of air/light), ceramide barrier repair creams, DMAE, hyaluronic acid in aqueous bases. Tailored for Puerto Rico's sun-intense environment.

**Slide 9 — Psoriasis and Eczema Compounding**

## MODULE 3 — Topical Anesthetics (BLT & Variants)

Create an educational presentation titled **"Compounded Topical Anesthetics: BLT and Beyond"** for dentists, aesthetic practitioners, dermatologists, and tattoo professionals in Puerto Rico.

**Slide 1 — Title**
"Topical Anesthetics: Compounded Solutions for Pain-Free Procedures" | Santa Cruz Compounding Academy

**Slide 2 — Why Compounded Topical Anesthetics?**
Commercial products have fixed concentrations. Compounding provides: higher or precisely titrated concentrations for deeper anesthesia, custom combinations, gels vs. creams for specific applications (mucosal vs. intact skin), cost-effective quantities for practice use.
**2024 FDA Safety Update:** In March 2024, the FDA warned consumers and providers about high-concentration OTC topical analgesic products marketed for cosmetic procedures (tattoo, laser hair removal, microdermabrasion, piercing). FDA issued warning letters to six companies. **Key risk:** High-concentration topical anesthetics applied under occlusion over large skin areas can cause irregular heartbeat, seizures, and respiratory failure. **Compounded BLT requires a valid prescription and must be dispensed with clear application instructions.** Unsupervised OTC BLT is prohibited — this is a key differentiator for legitimate compounding pharmacy practice. Additionally, November 2024, FDA issued a warning letter specifically against unapproved OTC BLT formulations (Centura Pharmaceuticals case). BLT prepared at a licensed 503A pharmacy by prescription remains a legitimate, regulated product.

**Slide 3 — The BLT Formula Explained**
BLT = Benzocaine + Lidocaine + Tetracaine. Standard ratio: 20% benzocaine / 6%–8% lidocaine / 4% tetracaine in cream or gel base. Mechanism of each component: Benzocaine — ester, rapid surface onset. Lidocaine — amide, intermediate depth and duration. Tetracaine — ester, slow onset but deepest penetration and longest duration. Synergistic combination = faster, deeper, longer anesthesia.

**Slide 4 — Application Guide for BLT**
Apply 1–2 mm thick layer under occlusion (cling film) for 30–60 minutes before procedure. Onset: 20–30 minutes surface; 45–60 minutes deeper tissue. Procedures: laser resurfacing, microneedling, botulinum toxin, fillers, electrolysis, tattoo. **Remove before laser** to prevent thermal concentration under cream. **Do not apply to broken, irritated, or compromised skin.** Apply to the minimum skin surface area required for the procedure. Maximum lidocaine dose: 4.5 mg/kg (plain) or 7 mg/kg (with epinephrine).

**Slide 5 — EMLA-Type (Lidocaine + Prilocaine Eutectic Mixture)**
How the eutectic mixture lowers melting point to create liquid at room temperature. Standard: 2.5% lidocaine + 2.5% prilocaine. Compounding advantage: adjust ratios, increase concentrations for longer procedures. Application: intact skin 1–2 hours; mucosa 5–15 minutes. Pediatric note: prilocaine causes methemoglobinemia — avoid in infants <3 months.

**Slide 6 — Mucosal Anesthetics for Dental and GI Use**
Viscous lidocaine 2% for oropharyngeal and esophageal procedures. Compounded benzocaine 20% oral gel for dental pre-injection anesthesia. Oral rinse formulas for mucositis. **Pediatric caution: benzocaine is contraindicated in infants under 2 years** — methemoglobinemia risk. This is an FDA black box warning for OTC benzocaine teething products.

**Slide 7 — Safety Profile: Methemoglobinemia Risk**
Causative agents: benzocaine and prilocaine (NOT lidocaine). At-risk populations: G6PD deficiency, infants <6 months, patients on oxidizing drugs (dapsone, nitrates, sulfonamides). Signs: cyanosis, chocolate-brown blood on phlebotomy, SpO2 falsely normal (pulse oximetry unreliable). Treatment: **methylene blue 1–2 mg/kg IV**. PABA metabolite cross-reactivity among ester anesthetics.

**Slide 8 — How to Order Compounded Topical Anesthetics at Santa Cruz**
Prescription required for all formulations. Available: BLT cream, BLT gel, lidocaine gel 4%–6%, EMLA-type cream. Custom concentrations on request. Turnaround 24–48 hours. Contact: (787) 798-4646 | santacruzpharmacare.com.

*Tone: clinical and safety-focused. Style: cool blue palette. Format: 16:9 presentation.*

## MODULE 4 — Pediatric Compounding

Create an educational presentation titled **"Pediatric Compounding: Medications Made for Children"** for pediatricians, general practitioners, pharmacists, and parents in Puerto Rico.

**Slide 1 — Title**
"Pediatric Compounding: Every Child Deserves the Right Dose, Right Form, Right Flavor" | Santa Cruz Compounding Academy

**Slide 2 — The Pediatric Medication Gap**
Many FDA-approved drugs lack pediatric formulations. Children are not small adults — pharmacokinetics differ fundamentally (different hepatic enzyme maturity, renal clearance, body composition). Problems with giving children adult tablets: inaccurate splitting, swallowing difficulty, poor compliance, toxicity risk. Compounding solution: custom liquid suspensions, chewables, gummies at the exact dose. **2025 Update (ASHP Standardize 4 Safety Initiative):** ASHP recommends standardizing to ONE concentration per drug when possible during care transitions to prevent dose-concentration confusion errors.

**Slide 3 — Weight-Based Dosing Calculations**
Three methods: (1) mg/kg dosing — most common. Example: amoxicillin 40 mg/kg/day ÷ 3 doses. (2) Clark's Rule: (weight in lbs ÷ 150) × adult dose. (3) Young's Rule: (age ÷ age + 12) × adult dose. Worked example: 8-year-old, 25 kg, needing omeprazole 10 mg/dose → volume of compounded 2 mg/mL suspension = 5 mL per dose.

**Slide 4 — Oral Suspensions and Syrups**
Suspending agents: OraSweet, OraPlus, methylcellulose, hydroxypropyl cellulose. Technique: levigation of API, geometric dilution, homogenization.
**BUD under USP 795 (2023 revised):**
- Aqueous preparations (aW ≥ 0.60): **14 days refrigerated; 35 days frozen**
- If extended with stability data and antimicrobial effectiveness testing: **up to 180 days**
Labeling: "Shake well before use," storage requirements, measuring device (oral syringe, never household teaspoon). Attach measuring syringe to every dispensed bottle.

**Slide 5 — Flavoring Science: Making Medicine Taste Good**
Flavor masking for bitter APIs (metronidazole, prednisolone, clindamycin). FlavoRx and FlavorPlus flavoring systems. Popular flavors for Puerto Rican pediatric patients: mango, parcha (passion fruit), strawberry, bubblegum, watermelon. Alcohol-free systems required for infants and toddlers. **2025 KIDs List (PPA):** Pharmacist must screen formula excipients for potentially inappropriate drugs in pediatrics: benzyl alcohol/sodium benzoate (gasping syndrome in neonates), propylene glycol (CNS toxicity in infants), polysorbate 80, phenylalanine, methyl/propylparabens.

**Slide 6 — Chewable Tablets and Gummy Formulations**
Chewable bases: mannitol, sorbitol, lactose. Gummy base: gelatin or pectin (vegan). Dose uniformity challenges in solid forms — require USP 905 weight variation testing. Puerto Rico humidity consideration: gummies need desiccant packaging and refrigerated storage.

**Slide 7 — Common Pediatric Compounds at Santa Cruz**
Examples: Prednisolone 5 mg/5 mL oral solution (mango). Omeprazole 2 mg/mL suspension (strawberry). Rifampin 25 mg/mL suspension (for TB prophylaxis). Gabapentin 100 mg/5 mL liquid (grape). Clonidine 0.05 mg/mL oral solution (bubblegum). **Note per ASHP 2025 Standardize 4 Safety:** Use one standard concentration per drug; document primary literature support or stability data.

**Slide 8 — Safety Considerations in Pediatric Compounding**
Excipient toxicity in infants (from 2025 KIDs List): benzyl alcohol — gasping syndrome in neonates (AVOID); propylene glycol — CNS toxicity in infants (LIMIT); sorbitol — osmotic diarrhea. Alcohol-free formulas required in pediatric populations. Mandatory second-pharmacist verification for all pediatric compounds. Weight-based dose double-check before dispensing.

## MODULE 6 — Hormones & BHRT Introduction

Create an educational presentation titled **"Bioidentical Hormone Therapy: Personalized Balance for Men and Women"** for OB/GYNs, endocrinologists, internists, and pharmacists in Puerto Rico.

**Slide 1 — Title**
"Bioidentical Hormone Therapy: Compounded Solutions for Hormonal Balance" | Santa Cruz Compounding Academy

**Slide 2 — What Are Bioidentical Hormones?**
Hormones whose molecular structure is identical to hormones naturally produced by the human body. Derived from plant sterols (diosgenin from wild yam and soy). Contrast with synthetic hormones: conjugated equine estrogens (Premarin = animal-derived), synthetic progestins (medroxyprogesterone, MPA) — structurally non-identical to progesterone.
**Important regulatory context:** The British Menopause Society (reviewed April 2026), ACOG, and other international bodies distinguish between **regulated bioidentical HRT (rBHRT)** (FDA-approved, plant-derived estradiol, micronized progesterone — e.g., Estrace, Prometrium) and **compounded bioidentical HRT (cBHRT)**. FDA-approved rBHRT is evidence-based; cBHRT fills gaps when patients need custom doses, combinations, or alternative delivery forms not available commercially.

**Slide 3 — Key Hormones in the BHRT Toolkit**
Estrogens: E1 (estrone), E2 (estradiol — most potent), E3 (estriol — weakest, lowest systemic absorption, preferred for GSM in breast cancer survivors). Common compounded blends: Bi-Est (E2 80% + E3 20%) and Tri-Est (E1/E2/E3). Progesterone (P4): micronized, metabolizes to allopregnanolone (GABA-A agonist → calming, sleep-promoting). Testosterone: important for women's libido, energy, cognition, bone. DHEA: adrenal androgen precursor, systemic and intravaginal.

**Slide 7 — Vaginal and Suppository Preparations**

Minimal systemic absorption — suitable for breast cancer survivors **with prescriber and oncologist guidance** (per NAMS 2022 position statement). DHEA intravaginal: locally converts to estrogen and testosterone within vaginal tissue. Compounded progesterone vaginal suppository for luteal support in fertility treatment.

**Slide 8 — Testosterone for Women: The Overlooked Hormone**

## MODULE 7 — General Compounding Principles (Reference Document)

Create a comprehensive reference document titled **"General Compounding Principles: Quality, Documentation and Calculations"** for pharmacy staff, compounding technicians, and pharmacists at Santa Cruz Pharma Care.

**Section 1 — Beyond-Use Dating (BUD): USP 795 Revised 2023**
Critical distinction: BUD (assigned by compounder) vs. expiration date (manufacturer). **Updated USP 795 (November 1, 2023) BUD Table — now based on water activity (aW):**

| Type of Preparation | aW | BUD | Storage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aqueous (emulsions, gels, creams, solutions, sprays, suspensions) | ≥ 0.60 | 14 days | Refrigerated |
| Aqueous | ≥ 0.60 | 35 days | Frozen |
| Non-aqueous (anhydrous) | < 0.60 | 90 days | CRT |
| Non-aqueous (anhydrous) | < 0.60 | 180 days | Refrigerated |
| Solid (capsules, tablets, powders, lozenges, suppositories) | Typically < 0.60 | 180 days | CRT |
| With CNSP-specific stability data + antimicrobial effectiveness testing | Any | Up to 180 days | Per stability study |

USP 795 Table 3 provides aW values for common compounded dosage forms — compounders may use this table to infer aW without direct measurement. Factors that reduce BUD: light-sensitive APIs (amber containers), moisture-sensitive ingredients, oxidation-prone compounds (vitamin C, tretinoin), known ingredient expiration dates earlier than calculated BUD.

**Section 2 — USP 797 (2023) Category-Based BUD Reference**

| Category | Environment | BUD |
|---|---|---|
| Category 1 CSP | ISO 5 PEC in uncontrolled segregated area | 12h RT / 24h refrigerated |
| Category 2 CSP (sterile start, aseptic) | ISO 5 PEC in ISO 7 buffer room + ISO 8 anteroom | Up to 90 days (with sterility testing) |
| Category 2 CSP (non-sterile start, aseptic, no sterility testing) | Same as above | 1 day RT / 4 days refrigerated / 45 days frozen |
| Category 3 CSP (sterility tested, full monitoring) | ISO 7 buffer + ISO 8 ante + sterility garb + weekly environmental sampling | 60–180 days |

**Section 3 — Master Formula Record (MFR) and Compounding Record (CR)**
Required MFR elements: formula name, preparation type, ingredient names and quantities, equipment list, step-by-step instructions, physical description (color, odor, appearance, pH), packaging, storage, QC procedures, BUD, labeling. CR elements: MFR reference, date, compounder initials, verifier initials, lot numbers of each ingredient, equipment calibration records, QC results. **Retention: minimum 2 years in Puerto Rico per Junta de Farmacia rules.**

**Section 4 — In-Process Quality Control**
Weight variation for capsules and suppositories: weigh 10 units individually, calculate mean and % deviation. Acceptance criteria per USP 905: no more than 2 of 10 units deviate >10% from mean; no unit >25%. pH measurement for aqueous preparations: calibrate with two buffers before use, record. Visual inspection for creams/gels: homogeneity, absence of lumps, correct color and odor. Content uniformity (USP 905) required if API is <25 mg or <25% of unit weight — applies to low-dose hormone capsules. Final labeling review before dispensing.

**Section 5 — Compounding Calculations Review**
Percentage strength (w/w, w/v, v/v). Alligation medial and alternate. Dilution and concentration problems. Specific gravity corrections. Aliquot method for small quantities below minimum weighable quantity (MWQ) of balance: MWQ = 5% × smallest readable increment. Example: analytical balance readability 0.1 mg → MWQ = 2 mg; API doses <2 mg require aliquot method. Practice problems: hormone formulas, pediatric suspensions, topical preparations.

**Section 6 — Packaging, Labeling, and Patient Counseling**

Visual comparison table of all 8 capsule sizes with approximate volumes and fill weights. Rule: select the **SMALLEST capsule that contains the required fill weight** — overfilling causes cap failure; underfilling causes dose inaccuracy.

**Slide 4 — Pack Stat: What It Is and Why It Matters**
Pack Stat = (Tapped density ÷ Bulk density) × 100. Predicts how much a powder compresses when packed — directly determines fill capacity of a given capsule size. Without Pack Stat: risk of wrong capsule size selection, non-closing caps, underfilled capsules, and failed weight variation testing.

**Slide 5 — Pack Stat Measurement: Step-by-Step**
Materials: 10 or 25 mL graduated glass cylinder, analytical balance, tapping apparatus (or USP-defined manual tap: 300 taps from 1-inch height). Step 1 — Bulk density: weigh exactly 5 g of formula → pour into cylinder → record volume → Bulk density = 5 g ÷ volume (mL). Step 2 — Tapped density: tap 300 times → record new volume → Tapped density = 5 g ÷ tapped volume. Step 3 — Pack Stat = (Tapped ÷ Bulk) × 100. Step 4 — Fill weight = capsule volume × tapped density. Worked example: progesterone 100 mg + lactose blend in size 0.

**Slide 6 — From Pack Stat to Capsule Size: Decision Workflow**
(1) Determine total dose weight (API + diluent). (2) Measure Pack Stat for the blend. (3) Calculate actual fill capacity = capsule volume × tapped density. (4) Select smallest capsule where capacity ≥ dose weight. (5) Validate with 10-capsule test fill before full batch. Diluent Pack Stat properties: MCC (high, compresses well), lactose monohydrate (moderate), dicalcium phosphate (low, dense).

**Slide 7 — Manual Capsule Filling**
Equipment: Torpac 100-hole, 50-hole, or 24-hole boards. Procedure: separate bodies/caps; spread blend across board; tamp 2–3 times with card; visual inspection (level fill, no air gaps); replace caps with firm uniform pressure; weigh 10 capsules for weight variation; wipe exterior with dry gauze. Static charge management: anti-static mat, humidifier, or charge-neutralizing bar in low-humidity environments.

**Slide 8 — Semi-Automatic Capsule Filling**
Auger fill head or tamping pin mechanism. Higher batch capacity (300–1000 capsules/run), improved uniformity, reduced fatigue. Validation runs: fill 20 capsules → RSD ≤3% acceptance (vs. ≤6% manual). Cleaning for hormone formulas: full disassembly of auger/tamping mechanism, brush cleaning, IPA wipe, verification wipe test. Dedicated equipment for USP 800 hazardous compounds.

**Slide 9 — Weight Variation Testing and QC (USP 905)**
Procedure: weigh 10 individual capsules → calculate mean → calculate % deviation of each from mean. **Acceptance criteria (USP 905):** no more than 2 of 10 units deviate >10% from mean AND no unit deviates >25% from mean. **For hormone capsules (narrow therapeutic index):** target RSD ≤5%. **Content uniformity (USP 905):** required when API <25 mg or <25% of unit weight — applies to low-dose hormone capsules (e.g., estradiol 0.5 mg in size 2 capsule). Document individual weights in CR, calculate mean and RSD, pharmacist verification sign-off.

**Slide 10 — Capsule Compounding Quality Summary Checklist**
Pre-compounding: Pack Stat measured, capsule size confirmed, API and diluents weighed on analytical balance (lot numbers recorded), blend ≥5 minutes. During: weigh every 20th capsule for trend monitoring. Post: weight variation/content uniformity on 10 units, visual inspection all capsules, polish exterior, package in HDPE child-resistant container, assign BUD (**180 days CRT for solid nonaqueous per USP 795, 2023**), enter CR, pharmacist verification before dispensing.
